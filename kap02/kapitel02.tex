\section{Stochastic Optimization and Chance Constraints}
\label{ch:background}

Chapter 1 established two observations that this chapter takes as its
starting point. The chance constraint is a natural mathematical language
for reliability requirements, because its protection level is an explicit
parameter under the decision maker's control. And it is hard to enforce
for exactly three structural reasons: a generally non-convex feasible
set, a probability with no closed form, and an indicator function with no
usable derivative. What Chapter~1 motivated, this chapter equips. The
argument proceeds in a deliberate order, because each piece is the
precondition of the next. \Cref{sec:taxonomy} fixes the taxonomy of
optimization under uncertainty, since the claim that chance constraints
occupy a unique position can only be defended once the alternatives are
stated precisely. \Cref{sec:classical} develops the classical theory and
its convexity islands, establishing both how much structure the field has
secured and how little of it survives contact with realistic
distributions. \Cref{sec:methods} then confronts the consequence: six
approximation methods, surveyed in a fixed rhythm of core idea, main
advantage, and primary limitation, each method responding to a
limitation of the one before it. The survey includes the blackbox approach, the most
assumption-free option of all and the deliberate starting point of the
methodological development in Part~3. With the landscape mapped and its
gap identified, \cref{sec:kde} introduces the instrument this thesis uses
to fill it, kernel density estimation, built up from first principles
because optimization readers will rarely have met it as an optimization
tool. \Cref{sec:litmap} closes the chapter by organizing the literature
that has already combined KDE with chance constraints into a single
narrative, whose one open end is the work of this thesis.

\subsection{A Taxonomy of Optimization Under Uncertainty}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

This section organizes the main paradigms of optimization under
uncertainty along two axes, the distributional information a model
consumes and the protection its solution provides. It first
characterizes the four classical answers and then places them on a
single conceptual map, which locates the position that chance-constrained
programming occupies.

\subsubsection{Four paradigms}
\label{ssec:paradigms}

Every approach to optimization under uncertainty answers the same two
questions: how much information about the random parameter $\xi$ the
model consumes, and how much protection against $\xi$ the solution
provides. These two axes are more than a convenience of exposition; they capture
much of how the field divides in practice. Four canonical answers
organize the classical landscape, and fixing them precisely now supports
the position claimed for chance constraints in \cref{ssec:map}.
 
\emph{Deterministic optimization} consumes the least information and
provides the least protection. The random parameter is replaced by a
single representative value, almost always its mean or a nominal
forecast, and the problem is solved as if that value were certain. The
formulation, its systematic failure mechanism, and the near $50\%$
violation rates it produces were dissected at length in Chapter~1 and
need no repetition; in this taxonomy it serves as the zero point against
which every other paradigm is measured.
 
\emph{Robust optimization} consumes set information: the modeler
specifies an uncertainty set $\Xi$ believed to contain every relevant
realization, and feasibility is enforced for all of them simultaneously,
$g(x,\xi) \leq 0$ for all $\xi \in \Xi$ \citep{soyster1973,bental2009}.
The resulting problems are minimax in character, and a mature duality
theory converts many of them into tractable deterministic equivalents.
Protection is maximal over $\Xi$, but it is an all-or-nothing property:
every point of the set is defended at equal priority, including points of
vanishing probability, and the objective pays for all of them.
 
\emph{Stochastic programming} consumes the full distribution and
optimizes in expectation. In its two-stage form, a here-and-now decision
$x$ is taken before $\xi$ is revealed and a recourse decision $y(\xi)$
repairs the outcome afterwards, with the objective
$f(x) + \E[\,Q(x,\xi)\,]$ where $Q$ is the optimal recourse cost
\citep{dantzig1955,birge2011}. The paradigm is the natural choice when
violations are not failures but costs: a shortfall that can be bought
back at a price belongs in the objective, not in a constraint. Its
protection statement is correspondingly soft. The solution is good on
average, but nothing bounds the probability that any individual
realization behaves badly.
 
\emph{Chance-constrained programming}, the framework this thesis lives
in, also consumes the full distribution (or samples from it) but spends
that information on a hard probabilistic guarantee rather than an
average: $\Prob(g(x,\xi) \leq 0) \geq 1-\varepsilon$
\citep{charnes1959,prekopa1995}. Where stochastic programming prices
violations, chance constraints ration them, and the ration $\varepsilon$
is chosen by the decision maker. This is the correct paradigm when a
violation is a discrete failure event whose frequency, not whose average
cost, is regulated: a lander crash, a load-shedding event, a breach of a
risk mandate.

\subsubsection{A conceptual map and where this thesis sits}
\label{ssec:map}

\Cref{fig:taxonomy} arranges the four paradigms on the two axes just
described. Reading the map yields three observations that recur
throughout the thesis. First, the paradigms are not competitors on a
single scale of sophistication; they occupy different points of a
two-dimensional trade-off, and each is the right tool somewhere, as
Chapter~1 acknowledged for the two extremes. Second, chance-constrained
programming is the only paradigm whose protection level is a continuous
dial rather than a fixed point: sweeping $\varepsilon$ from $0.5$ toward
$0$ moves the solution continuously from mean-value behavior toward
worst-case behavior over the support. Third, the dial comes at the price
identified in Chapter~1: the probability operator destroys the convexity,
the evaluability, and the smoothness that the other three paradigms
retain, which is why the approximation methods of \cref{sec:methods}
exist at all.

Two boundary remarks complete the map. Hybrid formulations exist and are
actively studied: distributionally robust chance constraints enforce the
probabilistic guarantee for every distribution in an ambiguity ball
around the empirical law, combining the chance-constrained dial with
robust-style protection against estimation error
\citep{chen2024,liu2022}. They reappear in \cref{ssec:tradeoffs} as a row
of the comparison table. And the boundaries between paradigms are
formulation choices, not laws of nature: the same engineering problem can
often be written in several of them, and part of the modeler's craft is
recognizing which paradigm matches the actual reliability language of the
application. The applications of this thesis, surveyed in Chapter~1, all
speak in failure probabilities, which fixes the choice.

\subsection{Classical Chance-Constrained Programming}
\label{sec:classical}

The taxonomy assigned chance-constrained programming its position; this
section establishes its content. The order of business is the one any
mature theory imposes: first the canonical formulations and the
equivalent forms the rest of the thesis computes with, then the strongest
structural result the field possesses, and finally an honest accounting
of how far that result reaches. The accounting matters, because the
methods of \cref{sec:methods} exist precisely in the territory the
classical theory does not cover, and the size of that territory is the
size of the problem this thesis addresses.

\subsubsection{Origins and canonical formulations}
\label{ssec:origins}

Chance constraints emerged from a concrete scheduling problem. Planning
heating-oil production under uncertain demand, \citet{charnes1958} faced
requirements that no fixed plan could satisfy on every realization, and
drew a conclusion the field has built on since: a planning constraint
need not hold always, only sufficiently often, and the required frequency
belongs in the model as a first-class quantity. \citet{charnes1959}
formalized the resulting notion of chance-constrained programming,
\citet{charnes1963} supplied early deterministic equivalents for such
programs, and \citet{miller1965} extended the framework from individual
to joint constraints, recognizing that a system of requirements demands a
probability statement about the system, not about each row separately.
The decisive consolidation came with Pr{\'e}kopa, whose log-concavity
theory (\cref{ssec:logconcavity}) and monograph \citep{prekopa1995}
elevated a modeling device into a branch of mathematical programming
with its own structural theorems. The formulations fixed in Chapter~1 are
restated here in the notation used for the remainder of the thesis. With
decision $x \in X \subseteq \R^n$, random parameter
$\xi \in \R^d$ with law $\Prob$, constraint map $g$, and risk level
$\varepsilon \in (0,1)$, the individual and joint chance constraints read
\begin{equation}
  \Prob\bigl(g(x,\xi) \leq 0\bigr) \geq 1-\varepsilon,
  \qquad
  \Prob\bigl(g_r(x,\xi) \leq 0,\; r=1,\dots,m\bigr) \geq 1-\varepsilon .
  \label{eq:ccpair}
\end{equation}

A third, equivalent form deserves its own display because the
methodological chapters lean on it constantly. Writing the requirement in
terms of the violation event,
\begin{equation}
  \Prob\bigl(g(x,\xi) > 0\bigr) \leq \varepsilon ,
  \label{eq:violation}
\end{equation}
makes the safety interpretation direct: the optimizer may move $x$
anywhere it likes as long as the estimated probability of violation stays
below the budget $\varepsilon$. The violation form is also the form in
which sample-based methods naturally operate, since counting violations
among samples estimates the left side of \cref{eq:violation} directly.
Throughout Part~3, the scalar random variable $Y(x) = g(x,\xi)$
introduced in Chapter~1 carries this event: $\{g(x,\xi) > 0\}$ is exactly
$\{Y(x) > 0\}$, and every approximation method in this chapter is at
bottom a different way of estimating the upper tail of $Y(x)$ at zero.
 
The pair in \cref{eq:ccpair} conceals an asymmetry that practitioners
learn quickly. Individual constraints control each requirement in
isolation and say nothing about simultaneous satisfaction; the joint
constraint controls the system but is strictly harder, both statistically
(the event is an intersection) and computationally (the feasible set
geometry worsens). The classical bridge is Boole's inequality: enforcing
individual constraints at levels $\varepsilon_r$ with
$\sum_r \varepsilon_r \leq \varepsilon$ implies the joint constraint at
level $\varepsilon$, at a cost in conservatism that grows with $m$. The
sharper treatment of joint constraints through a scalar violation
statistic is deferred to Part~3, where it forms research question RQ4.

\subsubsection{Convexity islands: the log-concavity theory}
\label{ssec:logconcavity}

The single most important positive structural result in the field is due
to Pr{\'e}kopa. A measure is log-concave when the logarithm of its
density is concave, a class that includes the Gaussian, the uniform on a
convex set, the exponential, and many other standard laws. Pr{\'e}kopa's
theorem states that if $\xi$ has a log-concave distribution and the set
$\{(x,\xi) : g(x,\xi) \leq 0\}$ is convex, then the probability function
$p(x) = \Prob(g(x,\xi) \leq 0)$ is log-concave in $x$, and consequently
every upper level set $\{x : p(x) \geq 1-\varepsilon\}$ is convex
\citep{prekopa1971,prekopa1995,prekopa2003}. On this island, chance-constrained
programming is convex programming: local solutions are global, duality
holds, and mature solvers apply.

  \centering

  \caption{Pr{\'e}kopa's theorem and its boundary, computed for the
  bilinear constraint $\xi^{\top}x \leq 1$ in $n=2$. (a) For Gaussian
  $\xi$, a log-concave law, the probabilistic feasible set
  $\{x : p(x) \geq 0.9\}$ is convex, as the theory guarantees. (b) For a
  two-component Gaussian mixture, which is not log-concave, the level set
  $\{x : p(x) \geq 0.55\}$ develops the non-convex geometry anticipated

  \label{fig:levelsets}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:levelsets} makes the theorem and its boundary visible in a
computed example. For a Gaussian parameter and a bilinear constraint, the
$90\%$ level set of $p(x)$ is convex, panel (a). Replacing the Gaussian
by a two-component mixture, a minimal departure from log-concavity,
produces the non-convex level set of panel (b), the continuous analogue
of the union-of-half-spaces construction in Chapter~1. The figure
clarifies a question of attribution that matters for everything
downstream: the non-convexity here is not caused by the constraint
function, which is identical in both panels, but by the shape of the
distribution. The geometry of the feasible set is inherited from the
geometry of the law of $\xi$, and modifications on the constraint side
alone cannot restore what the distributional side has removed.
 
This inheritance is exactly why the log-concavity island, elegant as it
is, carries so little of the practical load. The distributions documented
in the application domains of Chapter~1, skewed and heavy-tailed forecast
errors, multimodal disturbance mixtures, heavy-tailed returns, are
precisely the laws that leave the log-concave class. The conclusion is
worth stating plainly: the positive theory applies where the uncertainty
is well behaved, and the uncertainty that motivates chance constraints in
the first place often is not. For the problems considered in this thesis,
the general case is therefore the typical case rather than an exception.

\subsubsection{The general case}
\label{ssec:generalcase}

Outside the islands, the three difficulties of Chapter~1 stand in full
force, and restating them in the violation-form language of
\cref{eq:violation} is more than housekeeping: the methods of the next
section are organized by which difficulty each one attacks, and the
restatement is the index of that organization. The feasible set
$\{x : \Prob(Y(x) > 0) \leq \varepsilon\}$ is in general non-convex, so
global optimality is out of reach and the realistic target is a
high-quality local solution from a nonlinear programming method. The
probability $\Prob(Y(x) > 0)$ has no closed form: it is an integral of an
often unknown density over a region that moves with $x$, so it must be
estimated, almost always from samples. And the natural sample estimate is
an average of indicator functions, a piecewise-constant object whose
gradient is zero almost everywhere, useless to exactly the solvers that
handle nonlinearity well.
 
The landscape of \cref{sec:methods} is best previewed as a matrix of
concessions. Each classical method buys back tractability by
conceding one of three things: \emph{generality} (the parametric
route assumes the distribution), \emph{exactness} (the convex
restrictions enforce a stronger constraint than required), or
\emph{smoothness} (the sample-enforcement routes hand the solver a
non-differentiable or combinatorial problem). To the best of our
knowledge, no classical method preserves all three at once, which
reflects where decades of research have located the trade-offs. The case
this thesis builds, beginning in \cref{sec:kde}, is that estimating the
density of $Y(x)$ with a kernel estimator concedes none of the three
outright, paying instead with a bias that can be controlled and, in the
biased-KDE construction, even directed to the safe side.

\subsection{Standard Approximation Methods and Their Trade-offs}
\label{sec:methods}

This section positions the thesis relative to the landscape of existing
approaches, and it is built as a progression rather than a catalogue.
Each method is presented in the same fixed rhythm, core idea, main
advantage, primary limitation, and each method enters the survey as the
field's answer to the limitation of the one before it: the scenario
approach removes the distributional assumption the Gaussian route cannot
survive without, sample average approximation recovers the slack the
scenario approach forfeits, the convex restrictions restore the
tractability that sample averaging destroys, quantile smoothing recovers
the exactness the restrictions surrender, and the blackbox approach
strips away the last remaining assumption and pays the full price of
doing so. The section closes with the comparison this ordering has been
building toward, first as a table and then as a computed experiment on a
problem where the truth is known exactly.

\subsubsection{Parametric and Gaussian reformulations}
\label{ssec:gaussian}

The core idea of the parametric route is to assume the law of $\xi$ and
exploit it analytically. The Gaussian case with a constraint linear in
$\xi$ is the showpiece. For $g(x,\xi) = \xi^{\top}x - b$ with
$\xi \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$, the residual
$Y(x) = \xi^{\top}x - b$ is itself Gaussian with mean $\mu^{\top}x - b$
and variance $x^{\top}\Sigma x$, and the chance constraint collapses to
an exact deterministic statement,
\begin{equation}
  \Prob\bigl(\xi^{\top}x \leq b\bigr) \geq 1-\varepsilon
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \mu^{\top}x + \Phi^{-1}(1-\varepsilon)\,
  \bigl\lVert \Sigma^{1/2}x \bigr\rVert_2 \leq b ,
  \label{eq:socp}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi^{-1}$ is the standard normal quantile
\citep{charnes1963,shapiro2014}. For $\varepsilon \leq 1/2$ the right
side of \cref{eq:socp} is a second-order cone constraint: convex, smooth,
and solvable at scale by every modern conic solver.
 
The advantages within its domain are substantial: exactness, convexity,
smoothness, and speed, simultaneously. This is why the Gaussian
reformulation dominates practice in fields such as chance-constrained
optimal power flow \citep{bienstock2014} and why it serves as the
reference baseline of the experimental chapters of this thesis. When the
Gaussian assumption holds, the problem identified in Chapter~1 simply
does not exist.
 
The limitation is that the construction rests entirely on the
distributional assumption. Each step of \cref{eq:socp} follows from the
normality of $Y(x)$, and Chapter~1 quantified what can happen when that
normality does not hold: a skewed law whose tail mass is underestimated
by a factor of seven turns a certified $0.5\%$ failure rate into an
actual rate above $1.5\%$. The reformulation offers no internal warning
in such cases; it reports the same certificate whether or not the
underlying assumption is met. Extensions to other named families
and to Gaussian-like laws exist, including analytic gradient formulae for
the probability function via spheric-radial decomposition
\citep{vanackooij2014}, but they relocate the assumption rather than
remove it.

\subsubsection{The scenario approach}
\label{ssec:scenario}

If the Gaussian route fails because of its assumption, the natural
response is to make none, and the scenario approach makes none with full
commitment: it abandons the distribution entirely and enforces the
constraint on data. Given independent samples $\xi_1,\dots,\xi_N$, the
chance constraint is replaced by $N$ hard deterministic constraints,
\begin{equation}
  g(x,\xi_i) \leq 0, \qquad i = 1,\dots,N .
  \label{eq:scenario}
\end{equation}
The randomness now lives in the optimization problem itself, whose
solution $x^{\star}_N$ is a random variable, and the theory asks how
likely that solution is to satisfy the original chance constraint.
 
The advantage is the strength of the answer. For convex problems,
\citet{calafiore2006} showed that
$N \geq \tfrac{2}{\varepsilon}\bigl(\ln\tfrac{1}{\beta} + n\bigr)$
samples suffice for $x^{\star}_N$ to satisfy the chance constraint at
level $\varepsilon$ with confidence $1-\beta$, and \citet{campi2008}
sharpened this to an exact, distribution-free feasibility certificate.
These are finite-sample, a priori guarantees of a kind almost no other
method offers, obtained without assuming anything about the law of $\xi$.
The implementation is also disarmingly simple: sample, then solve a
deterministic program.

The limitations are structural. The program grows with $N$, one hard
constraint per sample, so precisely the regime where the statistics are
comfortable is the regime where the optimization is large. The approach
cannot exploit the slack the chance constraint permits: it defends every
sampled scenario, including the $\lfloor \varepsilon N \rfloor$ worst
ones it is entitled to ignore, so it is conservative by construction and
grows more conservative as $N$ increases, a behavior the experiment of
\cref{ssec:tradeoffs} displays vividly. And the guarantees are proven for
convex problems; in the nonlinear optimal control settings of this
thesis, they do not transfer.

\subsubsection{Sample average approximation}
\label{ssec:saa}

The scenario approach is conservative because it defends samples the
chance constraint entitles it to ignore. Sample average approximation is
the correction: it keeps the samples but restores the slack, allowing
exactly the violation budget the constraint grants. The violation
probability in \cref{eq:violation} is replaced by its
empirical counterpart,
\begin{equation}
  \hat{v}_N(x)
  = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\ind\bigl\{g(x,\xi_i) > 0\bigr\}
  \;\leq\; \hat{\varepsilon},
  \label{eq:saa}
\end{equation}
where the surrogate level $\hat{\varepsilon}$ may be set below
$\varepsilon$ to buy statistical confidence. The estimator is unbiased
and the approach is statistically consistent: as $N \to \infty$,
solutions of the approximated problem converge to solutions of the true
one under mild conditions \citep{luedtke2008,pagnoncelli2009}. Unlike the
scenario approach, SAA is allowed to discard its worst samples, so it
tracks the true constraint rather than restricting it.
 
The advantage is exactly this statistical fidelity: \cref{eq:saa} is the
right quantity to compute, the empirical frequency of the event the
constraint speaks about, with tunable conservatism through
$\hat{\varepsilon}$ and well-developed asymptotic theory.
 
The limitation is that \cref{eq:saa} is computationally challenging. The
indicator makes $\hat{v}_N$ piecewise constant in $x$: its gradient
vanishes almost everywhere and fails to exist on the boundary, so
derivative-based nonlinear solvers receive no signal. The standard
remedy is to encode the counting with binary variables, $g(x,\xi_i) \leq
M z_i$, $\sum_i z_i \leq \lfloor \hat{\varepsilon} N \rfloor$,
$z_i \in \{0,1\}$, which is exact but converts the problem into a
mixed-integer program whose difficulty grows combinatorially with $N$
\citep{luedtke2008}. SAA thus poses a choice between a landscape with no
gradients and a program with $2^N$ corners, and neither option fits the
smooth optimal control pipelines this thesis targets.

\subsubsection{CVaR and convex restrictions}
\label{ssec:cvar}

SAA's failure is computational, not statistical, and the
convex-restriction route responds on exactly that front: it trades the
exact constraint for a stronger one whose enforcement is tractable,
accepting conservatism as the price of convexity. Its central instrument
is the conditional value-at-risk. For the residual $Y(x)$ and level
$\varepsilon$, the Rockafellar-Uryasev representation
\begin{equation}
  \CVaR_{1-\varepsilon}\bigl(Y(x)\bigr)
  = \min_{t \in \R}\;
  \Bigl\{\, t + \tfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\,
  \E\bigl[(Y(x) - t)_{+}\bigr] \Bigr\} ,
  \label{eq:cvar}
\end{equation}
defines the average of the worst $\varepsilon$ fraction of outcomes
\citep{rockafellar2000}. Since the conditional value-at-risk dominates
the value-at-risk, enforcing $\CVaR_{1-\varepsilon}(Y(x)) \leq 0$
implies $\Prob(Y(x) > 0) \leq \varepsilon$, so the CVaR constraint is an
inner approximation of the chance constraint, and
\citet{nemirovski2006} established it as the tightest convex one in a
natural family.
 
The advantages are convexity and safety in the same package. If
$Y(x)$ is convex in $x$, then so is the CVaR constraint, the
representation \cref{eq:cvar} is smooth after the standard reformulation
of the positive part, and every feasible point of the restriction is
provably feasible for the original chance constraint. Nothing in the
construction requires knowing the distribution; the expectation in
\cref{eq:cvar} is routinely replaced by a sample average, which preserves
convexity.
 
The limitation is the direction of the inequality. CVaR controls the
magnitude of violations, not merely their frequency, so it enforces more
than the chance constraint asks: a decision with rare but large
exceedances is rejected even when its violation probability is well
inside the budget. The conservatism is largest exactly where chance
constraints are most natural, under heavy-tailed residuals whose tail
mean is far from the tail quantile. The experiment of
\cref{ssec:tradeoffs} quantifies this gap on a skewed law: the CVaR
solution overshoots the target reliability by a visible, structural
margin that no amount of data removes.

\subsubsection{Quantile smoothing}
\label{ssec:quantile}

The survey has so far traded exactness for smoothness or smoothness for
exactness; the question it forces is whether a method can refuse the
trade. Quantile smoothing is the first demonstration that it can, and it
is the closest methodological competitor to the approach of this thesis.
Its starting point is the quantile equivalence. The chance constraint
holds exactly when the $(1-\varepsilon)$-quantile of the residual is
nonpositive,
\begin{equation}
  \Prob\bigl(Y(x) \leq 0\bigr) \geq 1-\varepsilon
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  Q_{1-\varepsilon}\bigl(Y(x)\bigr) \leq 0 ,
  \label{eq:quantile}
\end{equation}
and the empirical quantile, an order statistic of the residual samples,
estimates the left side of \cref{eq:quantile} consistently. The empirical
quantile is itself non-smooth in $x$, so \citet{penaordieres2020} smooth
it, constructing a differentiable sample-based approximation of
$Q_{1-\varepsilon}(Y(x))$ and embedding it in a sequential quadratic
programming framework with trust regions tailored to the resulting
nonlinear program.
 
The advantages mirror those claimed for KDE in Chapter~1:
distribution-free operation, smoothness, gradients that mainstream
nonlinear solvers consume, and statistical guarantees for the smoothed
estimator. The method demonstrates convincingly that the
smooth-sample-approximation paradigm works at scale, and its existence is
part of the evidence that this paradigm, rather than enforcement or
restriction, is the right one for nonlinear problems.
 
The difference, and the limitation relative to this thesis, is the object
being smoothed. Quantile smoothing approximates one functional of the
residual law, the $(1-\varepsilon)$-quantile, and its smoothing is tied
to that functional and that level. The KDE route estimates the residual
density itself, from which the CDF at zero, its gradient, and quantiles
at every level follow simultaneously in closed form; the same estimate
also supports the biased construction of Part~3, which tilts the entire
density surrogate to the safe side, a manipulation with no direct
analogue at the level of a single quantile. The two methods are siblings,
and the experimental chapters treat quantile smoothing as the fairest
competitor.

\subsubsection{The blackbox approach}
\label{ssec:blackbox}

Every method so far has exploited some structure: a distributional form,
convexity, an analytic constraint map. The final method exploits none,
and it closes the survey for two reasons that should be stated openly.
It is where the assumption-stripping logic of the progression
terminates, the limiting case in which nothing whatsoever is presumed
about the problem. And it is where Part~3 begins, because it is the
approach a practitioner with a simulator and no further structure would
try first, so its precise mode of failure is the correct motivation for
everything this thesis builds. The core idea is to treat the violation
probability as a blackbox. The estimator
$\hat{v}_N(x)$ of \cref{eq:saa} can be evaluated at any $x$ by running
the simulator $N$ times and counting failures; nothing about $g$, the
law of $\xi$, or the shape of the feasible set needs to be known.
Optimization then proceeds with methods that consume only function
values: penalty formulations driven by derivative-free solvers such as
Nelder-Mead or pattern search, surrogate-assisted search, or
grid-and-refine heuristics over low-dimensional decision spaces.
 
The advantage is universality. The blackbox approach applies to
discontinuous simulators, legacy codes, and constraint maps with no
analytic form, settings where every other method in this section is
inapplicable in principle. It is also honest: $\hat{v}_N(x)$ is an
unbiased estimate of the true violation probability, so the quantity
being thresholded is statistically the right one, with none of the
conservatism of scenarios or CVaR.
 
The limitations are what Part~3 is built to overcome, and they are worth
stating sharply because they motivate everything that follows. The
response surface seen by the optimizer is a staircase: piecewise
constant in $x$, with zero gradient almost everywhere and jumps of size
$1/N$ wherever a sample crosses the constraint boundary, all of it
corrupted by sampling noise of order $N^{-1/2}$. Derivative-free methods
tolerate this landscape but pay in evaluations, and their cost grows
rapidly with the dimension of $x$; in the optimal control problems of
this thesis, where $x$ is a discretized trajectory with hundreds of
components, the approach becomes computationally impractical. The
blackbox approach is statistically sound but offers the solver no
directional information.

\subsubsection{Trade-offs in one view}
\label{ssec:tradeoffs}

The progression is complete, and its lesson can now be stated as a
single claim the rest of the subsection substantiates: the classical
methods partition the desirable properties among themselves, and none
holds all of them at once. \Cref{tab:methods} condenses the survey into
the four properties that matter for this thesis: whether the method is
distribution-free, whether it hands a smooth problem to the solver,
whether it is conservative, and what kind of feasibility guarantee it
offers. The two KDE rows preview Part~3, and the distributionally robust
row records the hybrid paradigm noted in \cref{ssec:map}, which wraps a
Wasserstein ambiguity ball around the empirical law and obtains exact
reformulations with finite-sample guarantees at the price of additional
conservatism \citep{chen2024}.

decision can be computed in closed form. Five methods produce a capacity

appear clearly. The Gaussian reformulation converges to a value near

and additional data does not correct this because the error lies in the
assumption rather than in the estimate. The scenario approach climbs toward reliability one, drifting
further from the target as $N$ grows, the conservatism that defends
samples it is allowed to ignore. CVaR stabilizes near $0.978$, safe but
structurally above target, paying for tail magnitudes the constraint
never asked it to control. SAA and KDE both converge to the target, the
defining behavior of methods that estimate the constraint rather than
assume or restrict it; their curves are nearly indistinguishable here
because the quantity is a scalar quantile, but only the KDE decision was
produced by a smooth, differentiable construction, which is the property
that matters once the decision variable stops being a scalar.

Two further smooth routes deserve mention for completeness, although
they lie outside the comparison of \cref{tab:methods} because they
exploit structure that the data-driven setting of this thesis does not
assume. Polynomial chaos expansions \citep{xiu2002} propagate
uncertainty through smooth dynamics with high accuracy when the law of
$\xi$ is known and sufficiently regular, and spheric-radial
decomposition \citep{vanackooij2014} yields analytic gradients of the
probability function for Gaussian and Gaussian-like laws. Both are
strong alternatives within their domains of validity; a systematic
benchmark against them lies outside the scope of this thesis.
 
The experiment makes the gap this thesis addresses concrete. What
appears to be missing from the classical landscape is a method that is
simultaneously distribution-free, smooth, non-conservative by default
but conservative on demand, and equipped with convergence guarantees.
The two KDE rows of \cref{tab:methods} claim this combination. The
remainder of the chapter introduces the estimator behind the claim and
the literature that has already tested it.
\subsection{Kernel Density Estimation}
\label{sec:kde}

The survey ended on a specification: a method that is distribution-free,
smooth, non-conservative by default and conservative on demand, with
convergence guarantees. An estimator with many of the required
properties has been available for decades. Kernel density estimation is standard equipment in statistics and
largely unknown equipment in optimization, so this section introduces it
deliberately, from the definition through the choices a user must make,
with one organizing emphasis throughout: the property optimization cares
about and statistics rarely examines, the behavior of the estimator as a
function to be differentiated and embedded in a solver. Read with that
emphasis, the classical theory of this section is not background; it is
an inventory of exactly the assets and exactly the liabilities the
reformulation of Part~3 inherits.

\subsubsection{The Parzen-Rosenblatt estimator}
\label{ssec:parzen}

Given scalar samples $y_1,\dots,y_N$ drawn from an unknown density
$\rho$, the kernel density estimator of \citet{rosenblatt1956} and
\citet{parzen1962} is
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\rho}_h(y)
  = \frac{1}{Nh}\sum_{j=1}^{N} K\!\left(\frac{y - y_j}{h}\right),
  \label{eq:kde2}
\end{equation}
where the kernel $K$ is a nonnegative function integrating to one and
the bandwidth $h > 0$ sets the smoothing scale. The mechanism is
transparent: each sample is replaced by a small probability bump of width
proportional to $h$, and the bumps are averaged. The estimator is itself
a density, it inherits the smoothness of $K$, and its cumulative
distribution function is the average of integrated kernels,
$\hat{F}_h(y) = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_j
\mathcal{K}\bigl((y-y_j)/h\bigr)$ with
$\mathcal{K}(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{t} K(s)\,ds$, available in closed form
whenever $\mathcal{K}$ is. These are precisely the objects assembled in
the residual-space construction of Chapter~1.
 
The estimator is consistent under remarkably weak conditions: if
$h \to 0$ and $Nh \to \infty$ as $N \to \infty$, then $\hat{\rho}_h$
converges to $\rho$ in mean integrated squared error for essentially any
density of interest \citep{parzen1962,devroye1985}. No shape is assumed
and none is imposed; given enough data the estimator recovers skewness,
heavy tails, and multimodality alike. This distribution-free consistency
is the property that earns KDE its place in this thesis, since the
distributions of Chapter~1 are exactly the ones that defeat parametric
fits.
 
For the optimization context, one more elementary observation carries
disproportionate weight. The estimator \cref{eq:kde2} is a finite sum of
shifted, scaled copies of a single fixed function. When the samples
$y_j = g(x,\xi_j)$ depend on a decision variable, every analytic property
of the estimate, its value, its integral, its derivative with respect to
$x$, reduces by the chain rule to evaluations of $K$, $\mathcal{K}$, and
the derivatives of $g$. There is no inner optimization, no numerical
integration, and no nondifferentiable operation anywhere in the pipeline.
The statistical literature prizes \cref{eq:kde2} for its flexibility; this
thesis prizes it equally for being, computationally, a closed-form
object.

\subsubsection{Kernel choices}
\label{ssec:kernels}

The kernel determines the local shape of the bumps, and three choices
matter for this thesis. The Gaussian kernel
$K(u) = (2\pi)^{-1/2}e^{-u^2/2}$ is smooth everywhere, has the standard
normal CDF as its integrated kernel, and yields infinitely
differentiable estimates with convenient gradients; it is the default
choice throughout the unbiased developments of Part~3. The Epanechnikov
kernel $K(u) = \tfrac{3}{4}(1-u^2)_{+}$ is optimal for density
estimation in the mean integrated squared error sense
\citep{epanechnikov1969}; its compact support also makes it the natural
raw material for kernel modifications, since the entire kernel can be
relocated. The split-Bernstein kernels of \citet{keil2021} are piecewise
polynomial constructions engineered for a property neither classical
kernel has: their integrated kernels bound the indicator function from
the safe side, which is the seed of the biased, conservative
reformulation developed in Part~3.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{kap02/figures/fig_kernels.pdf}
  \caption{Kernels and their integrated forms. (a) The Gaussian and
  Epanechnikov kernels, both symmetric, and a biased kernel obtained by
  relocating the Epanechnikov support, the simplest illustration of the
  biasing idea formalized by the split-Bernstein construction of
  \citet{keil2021}. (b) The corresponding integrated kernels against the
  indicator they replace. Symmetric kernels smooth the step from both
  sides and can overestimate the safe probability; the biased kernel's
  integrated form sits on one side of the step (shaded gap), so the
  probability estimate it produces errs toward safety by construction.}
  \label{fig:kernels}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:kernels} displays all three kernels and, in panel (b), the
property that organizes Part~3. A symmetric kernel's integrated form
crosses the indicator at its jump: it underestimates the contribution of
safely negative residuals and overestimates that of violating ones, and
the net effect on the probability estimate can fall on either side. The
biased kernel's integrated form sits entirely on one side of the
indicator, so every sample's contribution errs in the same, safe
direction. Anticipating the formal treatment, this single picture is the
biased-KDE method: choose the kernel so that smoothing and conservatism
are the same act.
 
Beyond these three, classical theory offers a reassurance that simplifies
the rest of the thesis: the choice among standard symmetric kernels
affects estimation efficiency only marginally, with the Gaussian
sacrificing a few percent of efficiency relative to the Epanechnikov
optimum \citep{silverman1986}. The choice of kernel is therefore of secondary importance for
estimation quality; the bandwidth is the considerably more consequential
choice.

\subsubsection{Bandwidth and the bias-variance trade-off}
\label{ssec:bandwidth}

The bandwidth is the decisive choice. Small $h$ produces an estimate that
follows the samples closely: low bias, high variance, and a ragged
density whose spurious modes are artifacts of the particular draw. Large
$h$ produces a stable but blurred estimate: low variance, high bias, with
genuine features such as modes and sharp tails smeared away. The mean
integrated squared error makes the trade-off exact. For a symmetric
kernel and a twice-differentiable density, the asymptotic expansion
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{AMISE}(h)
  = \underbrace{\frac{R(K)}{Nh}}_{\text{variance}}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{\frac{h^{4}\,\mu_2(K)^2\,R(\rho'')}{4}}_{\text{squared
  bias}},
  \qquad
  R(\phi) = \int \phi(s)^2\,ds,\;
  \mu_2(K) = \int s^2 K(s)\,ds,
  \label{eq:amise}
\end{equation}
is minimized at a bandwidth of order $N^{-1/5}$, whose constant
depends on $R(K)$, $\mu_2(K)$, and the curvature term $R(\rho'')$,
giving the optimal rate $\mathrm{AMISE}(h^{*}) = O(N^{-4/5})$
\citep{silverman1986}.
\Cref{fig:bandwidth} shows both faces of the trade-off computed on the
bimodal residual law used throughout this thesis: panel (a) the visual
pathology of under- and oversmoothing on a single sample, panel (b) the
empirical bias-variance decomposition over $120$ replications, with the
V-shaped total error and its interior minimizer.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{kap02/figures/fig_bandwidth.pdf}
  \caption{The bandwidth trade-off, computed on the bimodal law used in
  the residual-space illustration of Chapter~1. (a) One sample of size
  $N=200$ estimated at three bandwidths: $h=0.05$ invents spurious
  structure, $h=0.90$ erases the two genuine modes, and $h=0.25$
  recovers the truth (dashed). (b) Empirical decomposition of the
  integrated error over $120$ replications: variance falls and squared
  bias rises in $h$, and their sum is minimized at an interior $h^{*}$,
  here close to the $O(N^{-1/5})$ prediction of \cref{eq:amise}.}
  \label{fig:bandwidth}
\end{figure}

Since $h^{*}$ depends on the unknown curvature $R(\rho'')$, practice
relies on data-driven rules. Silverman's rule of thumb,
$h = 0.9\,\min\bigl(\hat{\sigma},\, \mathrm{IQR}/1.34\bigr)\,N^{-1/5}$,
calibrates the constant to a Gaussian reference and is robust enough to
serve as the default in most software \citep{silverman1986,wand1995}. Scott's
rule, $h = \hat{\sigma}\,N^{-1/(d+4)}$ per dimension, generalizes the
same idea to multivariate estimation \citep{scott1992}.
Cross-validation and plug-in methods estimate the curvature term from
the data and adapt to non-Gaussian shapes at additional computational
cost. For density estimation as an end in itself, any of these rules is
adequate, and the near-optimal bandwidth of \cref{fig:bandwidth}(a) is
the value such rules produce on this sample.
 
A caveat must be placed on record here, because it grows into a research
question later. Every rule above minimizes density estimation error, the
AMISE of \cref{eq:amise}. Nothing in their derivation knows that the
estimate will be thresholded at zero, differentiated with respect to a
decision variable, and handed to a nonlinear solver as a constraint. In
the optimization context the bandwidth shapes the feasible region and
the gradient field, and the $h$ that best reconstructs the density need
not be the $h$ that best locates the optimizer. This distinction, AMISE
optimality versus optimization optimality, is the substance of research
question RQ3 and is studied experimentally in Part~4. A related caveat
concerns the tails: kernel estimates are least accurate in sparsely
sampled low-density regions, so risk levels stringent enough to push the
binding region into the tails demand larger samples, while moderate
levels keep it in the well-sampled bulk. This tail sensitivity is
problem dependent, and the experimental chapters report results across a
range of risk levels for that reason.

\subsubsection{Multivariate KDE and the curse of dimensionality}
\label{ssec:curse}

The estimator extends to $d$-dimensional samples by replacing the scalar
bandwidth with a bandwidth matrix $H$,
$\hat{\rho}_H(y) = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_j \lvert H\rvert^{-1/2}
K\bigl(H^{-1/2}(y - y_j)\bigr)$, and the AMISE analysis extends with it.
The conclusion, however, reverses sign. The optimal bandwidth scales as
$O(N^{-1/(d+4)})$ and the optimal error as $O(N^{-4/(d+4)})$
\citep{silverman1986,scott1992}: the convergence rate decays with every
added dimension, and the sample size required to hold accuracy constant
grows exponentially in $d$. \Cref{fig:curse} translates the rate into
numbers. Holding the accuracy achievable with $N=100$ samples in one
dimension fixed, five dimensions already demand on the order of $10^4$
samples and ten dimensions on the order of $10^6$. This is the curse of
dimensionality in a particularly visible form, and it is a fundamental
limitation of multivariate kernel estimation. Even for the multivariate
normal distribution, evaluating orthant probabilities requires dedicated
numerical methods \citep{genz2009}, which indicates how demanding the
general multivariate problem is.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{kap02/figures/fig_curse.pdf}
  \caption{The curse of dimensionality for kernel density estimation:
  sample size required to maintain the accuracy achievable with $N=100$
  in one dimension, under the AMISE rate $O(N^{-4/(d+4)})$. The required
  sample size grows exponentially in $d$, reaching roughly $10^{6}$ at
  $d=10$. This figure is the quantitative case for estimating the scalar
  constraint residual rather than the uncertainty vector itself.}
  \label{fig:curse}
\end{figure}

The reader has already seen the answer this thesis gives. The
residual-space construction of Chapter~1 never asks KDE to work in the
dimension of $\xi$: an individual chance constraint produces a scalar
residual whatever the dimension of the uncertainty, and the estimation
problem is one-dimensional by design, where the $O(N^{-4/5})$ rate is
available and a few hundred samples suffice. The curse retreats to one
clearly marked location, joint chance constraints, whose residual vector
has dimension $m$. Keeping that dimension out of the estimator, by
collapsing the residual vector through a scalar violation statistic, is
precisely the extension pursued as research question RQ4, and
\cref{fig:curse} is the reason it is worth pursuing.

\subsection{KDE in the Chance-Constrained Literature: A Literature Map}
\label{sec:litmap}

The combination of kernel density estimation and chance constraints is
no longer a proposal awaiting validation; it is a literature with a
decade of results across four application domains and, since 2025, a
convergence theory. What that literature has so far lacked is an
organizing narrative, and supplying one is a substantive step of the
argument rather than a convenience: when the papers are read as a single
development, it becomes visible that the engineering line and the
theoretical line have not yet been connected systematically, which is
the gap this thesis addresses. The narrative has three movements, displayed as a
timeline in \cref{fig:litmap}: independent invention, the safety turn,
and the arrival of theory.

\subsubsection{First constructions: process systems and aerospace}
\label{ssec:firstwave}

The data-driven construction enters the literature with
\citet{calfa2015} in process systems engineering. Facing production
planning problems whose right-hand-side uncertainty was known only
through historical data, the authors replaced the unknown distribution
with a kernel-smoothed estimate and derived deterministic reformulations
for both individual and joint chance constraints, demonstrating them on
industrial planning and scheduling cases. The paper established the
template every later work follows, samples to smoothed estimate to
deterministic constraint, and demonstrated that the resulting problems
are solvable with standard nonlinear programming machinery.

\citet{caillau2018} carried the construction into optimal control, the
setting of this thesis. Treating launcher ascent and the Goddard problem
with uncertain parameters, the authors reformulated the chance
constraints through kernel estimates and solved the resulting smooth
optimal control problems with indirect and direct methods, establishing
feasibility of the approach in exactly the trajectory-optimization
context where the scenario and SAA routes are least comfortable. The
aerospace line and the process systems line were developed independently,
and their convergence on the same construction from different needs is
early evidence that the construction is natural rather than contrived.

In parallel, the energy systems community arrived by a third route.
\citet{ciftci2017} and \citet{ciftci2019} built adaptive-KDE chance
constraints for microgrid energy management under renewable uncertainty,
and the dispatch line that followed \citep{wu2021,wu2023} pushed the
construction to multivariate kernels for joint constraints in
power-system scheduling. Three communities, three motivations, one
estimator: by the end of this first movement the empirical case was
broad, but every guarantee in it was inherited from generic KDE
consistency, with nothing said about the optimization problems built on
top.

\subsubsection{The safety line: biased kernel density estimation}
\label{ssec:safetyline}

The first movement shares an unexamined weakness: a symmetric kernel
smooths in both directions, so the estimated safe probability can exceed
the true one, and a solution certified at level $1-\varepsilon$ can be
infeasible for the original constraint. The contribution of
\citet{keil2020} and \citet{keil2021} is to make the smoothing itself
safe. Their split-Bernstein kernels are constructed so that the
integrated kernel bounds the indicator from the conservative side, as
illustrated in \cref{fig:kernels}(b); the resulting probability estimate
is biased low by design, and feasibility for the approximated constraint
implies feasibility for the true one up to the estimator's statistical
error. The method is demonstrated on chance-constrained soft lunar
landing solved by direct collocation, the benchmark this thesis adopts.

The follow-up work of \citet{keil2021warm} addresses the computational
cost that conservatism introduces, proposing a warm-start strategy that
solves a sequence of problems with decreasing bandwidth and switching
kernels, using each solution to initialize the next. The pair of papers
constitutes the primary algorithmic reference of this thesis: between
them they supply the safe reformulation, the kernel constructions, and
the practical solution strategy that Part~3 develops, dissects, and
extends.

\subsubsection{Networks, breadth, and the arrival of theory}
\label{ssec:theoryarrival}

The construction's breadth test came from infrastructure. Probabilistic
constraints on gas networks were studied by \citet{gugat2018}, who
treated stationary networks with compressor control under random loads.
Building on this line, \citet{schuster2022} applied KDE-reformulated
probabilistic constraints
to stationary gas networks and dynamic water contamination networks,
derived the gradients of the kernel-estimated probability analytically,
and used them in necessary optimality conditions, comparing the KDE
route against spheric-radial decomposition, the analytic alternative
available under Gaussian-type laws \citep{vanackooij2014}. Adjacent
developments multiplied the evidence: nonparametric prediction intervals
for wind power \citep{wan2020}, Bayesian nonparametric gas-power flow
\citep{wang2021}, KDE-based tail-risk portfolio optimization
\citep{liu2022}, KDE-driven distributionally robust load shedding
\citep{qi2026}, and Hilbert-space embeddings of nonparametric chance
constraints in robotics \citep{gopalakrishnan2018}.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{kap02/figures/fig_litmap.pdf}
  \caption{The KDE chance-constraint literature as a timeline, organized
  by application stream. The construction appears independently in
  process systems \citep{calfa2015}, aerospace \citep{caillau2018}, and
  energy systems \citep{ciftci2017,ciftci2019}; the biased-KDE safety
  line \citep{keil2020,keil2021,keil2021warm} and the network

  of \citet{schuster2025} arrives a decade after the first engineering

What every entry above still lacked was a statement about the
optimization problem itself: KDE consistency guarantees that the
estimated probability converges, not that the minimizer of the estimated
problem converges to the minimizer of the true one. \citet{schuster2025}
supplies exactly this missing piece, proving convergence of optimal
values and solution sets of the KDE-approximated chance-constrained
problem to those of the original as $N \to \infty$, under verifiable
conditions on the kernel, the bandwidth sequence, and the problem data.
With this result the literature acquires the shape visible in
\cref{fig:litmap}: a decade of engineering constructions, and a
convergence theory developed separately that certifies the class they
belong to.

The gap that remains is the connection itself, and it is the gap this
thesis occupies. The engineering line and the theoretical line have not
been brought into systematic contact: no work verifies the convergence
conditions for the biased constructions used in practice, no controlled
study quantifies how bandwidth choices made by AMISE rules behave inside
an optimizer, and no unified experimental comparison places the KDE
family against the classical methods of \cref{sec:methods} across sample
sizes, risk levels, and problem types, including the optimal control
setting. Building that bridge, formulation by formulation in Part~3 and
experiment by experiment in Part~4, is the work the next chapter begins,
starting where any practitioner would: with the blackbox.
