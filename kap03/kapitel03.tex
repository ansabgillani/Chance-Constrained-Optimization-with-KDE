\section{Smooth Approximation with Standard (Unbiased) KDE}
\label{ch:unbiasedkde}

Chapter~2 ended on a specification written by the failure of the
blackbox: a single deterministic constraint that approximates the safe
probability, depends smoothly on the decision, and carries an analytic
gradient. This chapter constructs that object. The construction has five
steps, and the order matters because each step is what makes the next
one possible: the change of variables that moves estimation into
residual space (\cref{sec:residualtransform}), the residual samples that
the change of variables produces (\cref{sec:residualsamples}), the
kernel density estimate built on them (\cref{sec:residualkde}), the
integrated-kernel identity that collapses the probability into a finite
smooth sum (\cref{sec:integratedkernel}), and the analytic gradient that
the identity makes available (\cref{sec:gradient}). With the object
built, \cref{sec:reformulation} assembles the full reformulated
nonlinear program, and \cref{sec:properties} states its properties with
the candor the thesis owes them, including the one property it lacks,
whose repair is the business of Chapter~5.

\subsection{The Residual-Space Transformation}
\label{sec:residualtransform}

The fundamental insight behind the KDE approach, previewed in Chapter~1
and made load-bearing here, is a change of the space in which
probabilities are estimated. The question the chance constraint
literally poses is geometric: what is the probability that $\xi$ lands
in the safe region $M(x) = \{\xi : g(x,\xi) \leq 0\}$, a subset of
$\R^d$ whose shape deforms with every candidate decision? The question
this thesis poses instead is scalar: what is the probability that the
constraint residual
\begin{equation}
  Y(x) \;=\; g(x,\xi) ,
  \label{eq:residual4}
\end{equation}
is non-positive? The two questions have the same answer, identically and
without approximation,
\begin{equation}
  \Prob\bigl(Y(x) \leq 0\bigr)
  \;=\; \Prob\bigl(g(x,\xi) \leq 0\bigr)
  \;\geq\; 1-\varepsilon ,
  \label{eq:equivalence}
\end{equation}
because the event $\{Y(x) \leq 0\}$ \emph{is} the event
$\{\xi \in M(x)\}$, written in the variable the constraint actually
speaks about.

What changes is not the answer but the tractability of computing it,
and the change is substantial rather than cosmetic. The original question demands
the integral of an unknown $d$-dimensional density over a moving region,
the second of the three difficulties catalogued in Chapter~1, with the
curse of dimensionality of Chapter~2 attached to any attempt to estimate
that density nonparametrically. The transformed question asks for a
one-dimensional cumulative distribution function evaluated at a fixed
point, zero. The dimension of $\xi$ no longer enters the estimation
problem: whether the uncertainty lives in two dimensions or two
hundred, the residual of an individual constraint is a scalar, and the
constraint map $g$ itself has performed the dimension reduction, without
information loss, because \cref{eq:equivalence} is an identity. This is
the construction \citet{schuster2025} places at the foundation of the
convergence theory, and it is the reason the KDE machinery of Chapter~2
can be deployed in exactly the one-dimensional regime where it is
strongest.

\subsection{Residual Samples}
\label{sec:residualsamples}

For a fixed decision $x$ and the samples $\xi_1,\dots,\xi_N$ of
Chapter~3, define the residual samples
\begin{equation}
  y_j(x) \;=\; g(x,\xi_j), \qquad j = 1,\dots,N .
  \label{eq:ressamples}
\end{equation}
These are realizations of the random variable $Y(x)$, obtained by
pushing each uncertainty sample through the constraint map at the
current decision, and they are the raw material of everything that
follows.
 
One property of \cref{eq:ressamples} must be understood precisely,
because it is the property that distinguishes this construction from
ordinary density estimation and shapes the analysis of the entire
thesis: the residual samples are functions of the decision variable.
The uncertainty samples $\xi_j$ are drawn once and frozen; the residual
cloud $\{y_j(x)\}$ moves whenever the optimizer moves $x$. Estimation
and optimization are therefore not sequential stages but a single
coupled process. The estimator is rebuilt, implicitly, at every iterate,
and the constraint the solver sees is not a fixed statistical object but
a family of them indexed by $x$. This coupling is what makes the
smoothness of the next two sections consequential rather than
decorative: a smooth estimator of a fixed density would be a statistical
convenience, while a smooth estimator of a moving one is a differentiable
constraint.

\subsection{KDE of the Residual Distribution}
\label{sec:residualkde}

The Parzen-Rosenblatt estimator of Chapter~2, applied to the residual
samples, estimates the density of $Y(x)$ as
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\rho}_h(z;x)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{Nh}\sum_{j=1}^{N}
  K\!\left(\frac{z - y_j(x)}{h}\right),
  \label{eq:reskde}
\end{equation}
with kernel $K$ and bandwidth $h > 0$ carrying the meanings and the
selection theory established there. The estimated probability that the
constraint is satisfied is the mass this density places on the safe
half-line,
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_h(x)
  \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{0} \hat{\rho}_h(z;x)\, dz .
  \label{eq:phat-integral}
\end{equation}
Two features of \cref{eq:phat-integral} should be recognized as the
direct payoff of the residual-space transformation. The integration
region is fixed: it is $(-\infty, 0]$ for every decision, every problem,
and every distribution, because all dependence on $x$ has migrated into
the samples. And the integrand is a known finite sum of kernel bumps,
not an unknown density, so the integral is not a numerical task but an
algebraic one, which the next section carries out.

\subsection{The Integrated Kernel Reformulation}
\label{sec:integratedkernel}

Substituting \cref{eq:reskde} into \cref{eq:phat-integral} and
integrating term by term against the integrated kernel
$F_K(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{t} K(s)\,ds$ yields the closed form
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\;
  \hat{p}_h(x)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}
  F_K\!\left(\frac{-\,y_j(x)}{h}\right)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}
  F_K\!\left(\frac{-\,g(x,\xi_j)}{h}\right)
  \;} \, ,
  \label{eq:integrated}
\end{equation}
and, for the Gaussian kernel, $F_K = \Phi$, the standard normal CDF, so
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_h(x)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}
  \Phi\!\left(\frac{-\,g(x,\xi_j)}{h}\right) .
  \label{eq:gaussiancase}
\end{equation}
The deterministic approximation of the chance constraint is then the
single smooth inequality
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_h(x) \;\geq\; 1-\varepsilon .
  \label{eq:smoothcc}
\end{equation}
 
\Cref{eq:integrated} is among the most important equations in
this thesis, and the claim deserves its justification. Set
\cref{eq:integrated} beside the indicator estimator of Chapter~3: the
two expressions are term-for-term identical except that the
discontinuous indicator $\ind\{g(x,\xi_j) \leq 0\}$ has been replaced by
the smooth sigmoid $F_K(-g(x,\xi_j)/h)$, exactly the substitution drawn
in Chapter~2's kernel figure. Every sample still votes on feasibility,
but the vote is no longer binary; a sample near the constraint boundary
contributes a graded value that responds continuously as the decision
moves. The staircase has been replaced by a ramp, and the entire
discontinuous probabilistic feasibility requirement of Chapter~3 by a
smooth, differentiable, deterministic nonlinear constraint. \Cref{fig:smoothing}(a) shows the
replacement on the dispatch problem: the staircase, the true
probability, and the KDE constraint at three bandwidths, with the
bandwidth visibly controlling how tightly the ramp hugs the step.

\subsection{Gradient Computation}
\label{sec:gradient}

Differentiating \cref{eq:integrated} by the chain rule, using
$F_K' = K$, gives the gradient in closed form,
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_x\, \hat{p}_h(x)
  \;=\; -\,\frac{1}{Nh}\sum_{j=1}^{N}
  K\!\left(\frac{-\,g(x,\xi_j)}{h}\right)\nabla_x\, g(x,\xi_j) .
  \label{eq:gradient}
\end{equation}
The formula repays a moment of reading, because its structure is
central to the mechanism of the method. The gradient is a kernel-weighted average
of the constraint gradients $\nabla_x g(x,\xi_j)$, and the weights
$K(-g(x,\xi_j)/h)$ are largest for the samples whose residuals lie
within a bandwidth of the boundary. The estimator automatically
concentrates the solver's attention on the scenarios that are about to
change status, which is exactly the directional information Chapter~3
showed the indicator withholds. For the Gaussian kernel every quantity
in \cref{eq:gradient} is elementary, second derivatives follow by the
same chain rule, and sequential quadratic programming and
interior-point methods receive exact first- and second-order
information rather than finite differences of a staircase
\citep{waechter2006}. \Cref{fig:smoothing}(b) displays the comparison
directly: the staircase derivative is
zero except at isolated undefined points, while the KDE derivative is a
smooth, analytically available function at every decision. This
differentiability is the core computational advantage of the KDE
reformulation over blackboxes.

\subsection{Stochastic Optimization Reformulated via Unbiased KDE}
\label{sec:reformulation}

Assembling the pieces, the chance-constrained program of Chapters~1
through~3,
\begin{equation}
  \minimize_{x \in X}\; f(x)
  \quad\text{subject to}\quad
  \Prob\bigl(g(x,\xi) \leq 0\bigr) \geq 1-\varepsilon,
  \label{eq:original4}
\end{equation}
becomes, after the KDE substitution,
\begin{equation}
  \minimize_{x \in X}\; f(x)
  \quad\text{subject to}\quad
  \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}
  F_K\!\left(\frac{-\,g(x,\xi_j)}{h}\right) \geq 1-\varepsilon .
  \label{eq:reformulated4}
\end{equation}
Problem \cref{eq:reformulated4} is a deterministic nonlinear program,
and its structure should be read off explicitly because it answers,
point by point, the specification Chapter~3 issued. One smooth nonlinear
inequality replaces one probabilistic constraint; the program does not
grow with the sample size, in deliberate contrast to the scenario
approach, because the samples $\xi_j$ enter as fixed parameters inside a
single sum. The decision variable enters only through the residuals
$g(x,\xi_j)$, so the constraint is exactly as smooth as the constraint
map itself. Standard solvers, IPOPT, SNOPT, and SQP implementations,
consume \cref{eq:reformulated4} directly, with the analytic derivatives
of \cref{sec:gradient} supplied through the modeling layer
\citep{waechter2006}. \Cref{fig:pipeline} draws the complete loop: the
frozen samples and the moving decision meet in the residuals, the KDE
turns the residuals into a smooth constraint value and gradient, and the
solver feeds the next iterate back into the residual map. This loop is
the computational object the remainder of the thesis studies, extends,
and stress-tests.

\subsection{Properties of the Unbiased KDE Approximation}
\label{sec:properties}

The reformulation \cref{eq:reformulated4} has four properties, and the
thesis states all four with equal directness because the fourth is a
genuine limitation. First, it is smooth: for kernels with differentiable
$F_K$, the Gaussian above all, the constraint is twice continuously
differentiable in $x$ wherever $g$ is, and the derivatives are the
closed forms of \cref{sec:gradient}. Second, it is consistent: as
$N \to \infty$ with $h \to 0$ and $Nh \to \infty$, the estimate
$\hat{p}_h(x)$ converges to the true probability
$\Prob(g(x,\xi) \leq 0)$, inheriting the classical consistency of the
kernel estimator \citep{parzen1962,silverman1986}, and under the
conditions of \citet{schuster2025} the convergence lifts from the
constraint to the optimization problem itself, optimal values and
solution sets included. It is instructive to separate the two error
sources at play, as anticipated in Chapter~3: a random sampling error of
order $N^{-1/2}$, inherited from the finite sample, and a deterministic
smoothing error controlled by the bandwidth, introduced by the kernel
\citep{silverman1986,wand1995}. The conditions $h \to 0$ and
$Nh \to \infty$ balance exactly these two contributions, and the
biased construction of Chapter~5 acts on the second source alone,
giving it a sign rather than removing it. Third, the bandwidth controls a genuine
trade-off: \cref{fig:smoothing}(a) shows small $h$ reproducing the
staircase together with its sampling noise and large $h$ distorting the
feasible boundary, so $h$ is not a nuisance parameter but a design
variable, the optimization-versus-AMISE question flagged in Chapter~2
and studied experimentally in Part~4.
 
Fourth, and importantly for the structure of this thesis, the
approximation is not conservative. A symmetric kernel smooths the
indicator from both sides, so in finite samples the estimate
$\hat{p}_h(x)$ can exceed the true probability, and the constraint
$\hat{p}_h(x) \geq 1-\varepsilon$ can certify a decision the true chance
constraint rejects. \Cref{fig:optimism} measures the consequence on the
dispatch problem: across three thousand independent trials at
$N = 100$, the decision certified by the unbiased KDE violates the true
reliability target in $39\%$ of trials. The failures are individually
small, the estimator is consistent and the misses concentrate near the
target, but they are systematic, and a reformulation whose certificate
fails in roughly four of ten trials functions as an accuracy method
rather than a safety method. For applications where $\varepsilon$ is a
hard requirement, a lander, a grid code, a risk mandate, accuracy alone
does not meet the requirement; a conservative guarantee is needed. Constructing a KDE reformulation that keeps every
property of this chapter while erring, by design, only on the safe side
is the contribution of the biased kernels of \citet{keil2021}, and it is
the business of the next chapter.
